The Evolution
of the Game Boy™
1989 Game Boy
The Nintendo Game Boy™ was released in Japan in the spring of 1989, and subsequently released in Europe and North America later that summer. Its predecessor, the Game & Watch, had long since paved the way for handheld gaming consoles. And much like its predecessor, the Game Boy would see unprecedented success. Among the titles released at its launch, Super Mario Land became a staple of the Nintendo company. Even for its time, the Game Boy was a relatively simple gaming console. Gameplay was controlled with the A button, the B button, and the control pad, and its screen displayed graphics in four shades of grey.
1998 Game Boy COLOR
The Game Boy Color, successor to the Game Boy, was the first Nintendo console to feature a coloured screen. Despite its likeness to its nine-years-predecessor, the Game Boy Color largely outsold its new competition. With its release came games such as Donkey Kong, Kirby's Dream Land, and Pokemon Red and Blue, establishing titles that would later become the Nintendo company's most popular franchises.
2001 Game Boy Advance
The Game Boy Advance was the third generation in the Game Boy series. Differing from the portrait screens of the previous generations, it featured a landscape screen, with significantly improved graphic capabilities. The demand for a backlit screen later gave rise to the Game Boy Advance SP, and shortly after, a compact model known as the Game Boy Micro was released. In addition to new titles, sequels of popular Game Boy Color titles were made excusively for the Game Boy Advance, of which Pokemon topped sales by an overwhelming margin.
2004 Nintendo DS
In 2004, Nintendo released the Nintendo DS, a dual-screen successor to the Game Boy Advance. It made strides in both audio and graphics, quickly becoming the best-selling handheld console in history. It was succeeded in 2011 by the Nintendo 3DS, a console capable of projecting 3D effects without the need for glasses. The Nintendo 3DS was popular among a wide demographic, attracting both young gamers and loyal Nintendo fans. Titles such as Animal Crossing: New Leaf became popular among mothers and daughters, while 3D predecessors to the Pokemon series saw a surge in popularity among college students and young adults. Yet, despite how far the Game Boy has come since 1989, through the Nintendo eShop, fans can still download and play their favourite Game Boy titles, in all four nostalgic shades of grey.
